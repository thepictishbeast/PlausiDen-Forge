Programs — Northbrook Observatory

Programs

What the dome is for.

Three programs share the same forty-inch reflector — public observing on the second Friday of every month, school programs October through May, and a small research portfolio that runs on the spare time between the other two.

Public observing.

Free to attend, no registration required, second Friday of every month. We open the doors at dusk, the dome opens to the sky at the end of nautical twilight, and we close when the last visitor leaves — typically 23:30 in summer and 22:00 in the depth of winter.

What we do
Three staff or volunteers rotate through three stations — the dome (looking through the eyepiece at whatever's high), a lawn telescope (a more accessible 8-inch Dobsonian for tonight's brightest target), and a sky-pointing host who runs binoculars and a star map.
What you should bring
Warm layers — even July nights run cold at this elevation. A red headlamp or red-filtered phone (we have spares to lend). A folding chair if you'd like to wait outdoors for the dome rotation. Curious questions.
What we ask
No white flashlights. No outside food in the dome (the stairs are narrow and we'd rather not clean up). Photography is welcome on the lawn but not inside the dome — the camera screens degrade everyone else's dark adaptation.
Weather
If forecast is decisively bad we cancel by 14:00 the day-of and post a notice on the website + send a cancellation email to the sky-report list. Partial cloud, light fog, or a Bortle-degrading moon does not cancel — we'll find something to point at.

School programs.

October through May, we host two school visits per month — typically a Tuesday afternoon for the daytime sun-and-equipment program and a Friday evening for night observing. Visits are free to public schools within Grafton, Coös, and Carroll counties. Schools further afield pay a $200 program fee that we put toward scholarships for closer schools.

What we offer

Solar program (daytime)
Hydrogen-alpha solar viewing through the lawn telescope + a 45-minute talk on stellar evolution suitable for grades 5-12. Indoors backup if weather doesn't cooperate.Best for grades 5-12
Night program (evening)
Dome observation + tour of the building + the same Q&A we run for public nights. We open the original 1888 floor display so students can see the wear pattern from the equatorial pier.Best for grades 7-12; flexible for younger with chaperones
Bring-your-own (any age)
If your class has been studying a specific phenomenon (Galilean moons, lunar phases, sunspots), tell us in advance and we'll point at it. Programs we cannot do: tides, atmospheric optics (no equipment).Tell us six weeks ahead
Bus access
Ridge Road is paved to within 200 metres of the gate. School buses can turn around in the parking field. Drivers — please call ahead so we can have someone meet you at the gate; signage on Ridge Road is intentionally minimal.Don't trust the GPS

Current research.

We run a small variable-star photometry program in collaboration with the AAVSO Bright Star Monitor (BSM) network and a hosting arrangement with two graduate students from UNH. Total telescope-time allocation is roughly 40 nights per year — the photometric ones — which leaves the cloudy nights for outreach and equipment maintenance.

AAVSO BSM Berry North
Second-site cross-check for the southern-hemisphere BSM Berry South campaign. Targets are 23 eclipsing binaries with century-scale period drift + 4 recurring novae the AAVSO would like tighter outburst detection on. We contribute photometry; AAVSO does the analysis.
UNH grad-student hosting
Two slots per academic year, one fall, one spring. Students bring their own science but use our dome and our pipeline. Recent projects: transit photometry of WASP-12 (Sarah K., 2024-25), Cepheid distance check for M33 RV-Tauri stars (Daniel L., 2025-26).
Citizen-science observing nights
Four per year, opt-in for sky-report subscribers. Visitors run a guided photometry session — typically reading magnitudes of a Mira-class variable across the night. Data is contributed to the AAVSO citizen-science archive with the participants credited.
What we don't do
Spectroscopy of bright stars (out of our aperture league for high-resolution work). Survey work (we have no autonomous queue manager). Anything time-critical to the minute (manual scheduling can't guarantee that). If your project needs those, we're not the site for you and we'll happily refer you to someone larger.
